The exponential growth in AI computation power has played a pivotal role in the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence over the decades. This growth is tracked through the amount of computational power required to train AI models, as depicted in a chart from Our World in Data.
<br/>
The history of AI computation can be divided into three eras. In the early stages, from the 1950s to 2010, compute power doubled approximately every 18 to 24 months, following Moore's Law. However, the Deep Learning Era, marked by the emergence of AlexNet in 2012, saw a significant acceleration, with compute doubling every 5 to 7 months. The third era, characterized by large-scale AI models like AlphaGo and GPT-3, required even more computational power, with doubling occurring every 11 months from 2016 to 2022.
<br/>
This tremendous growth in computational resources, combined with the availability of vast datasets and improved algorithms, has led to remarkable progress in AI, surpassing human performance in numerous domains. Notable AI models, such as AlexNet and GPT-3, have required exponentially increasing amounts of computation for training.
<br/>
However, sustaining this pace of growth poses challenges. Large-scale models demand increasingly more computational power, and the exhaustion of available training data could hinder further progress. Nevertheless, substantial investments in AI research and development offer hope for future breakthroughs, potentially approaching the computational capacity of the human brain.
<br/>
In summary, the exponential growth in AI computation power has driven rapid advancements in artificial intelligence, with compute doubling at an ever-accelerating rate. This trend, accompanied by better data and algorithms, has propelled AI to new heights, although maintaining this pace poses challenges that require ongoing innovation and investment.

# ***What are Retail Investors Interested in Buying in 2023?***
<br/>
<Image src="/What-Retail-Investors-Are-Focusing-On_Sep-12.jpg" alt="" width={700} height={650} />
<br />
In the world of rough diamond production, only 22 countries are actively engaged in mining these uncut gems from their natural deposits. Russia stands out as the leader in this industry, mining a staggering 42 million carats of rough diamonds in 2022, overshadowing its closest competitors. Botswana and Canada hold the second and third positions, with 24.8 million and 16.2 million carats, respectively. This hierarchy underscores Russia's dominance, despite challenges related to tracing the origin of diamonds in the face of sanctions.
<br/>
Notably, the top 10 diamond-producing nations by weight account for an overwhelming 99.2% of the global output, highlighting the concentration of this resource. However, the value of diamonds is not solely determined by carat weight. Factors like cut, color, and clarity play a significant role in a diamond's worth.
<br/>
When considering diamond production by value in 2022, Botswana emerges as a prime example. Despite producing just 59% of Russia's carat weight, Botswana's diamonds had a trade value of nearly $5 billion, surpassing Russia's by 1.5 times. Angola, ranking sixth in production but third in value, exhibits a similar trend. These countries, along with South Africa, Canada, and Namibia, are known for producing gem-quality rough diamonds, unlike Russia and the Democratic Republic of Congo, which primarily mine diamonds for industrial purposes.
<br/>
Regionally, Africa dominates the rough diamond production landscape, accounting for 51.4% of output by weight and 66.4% by value. This continent has a relatively recent history in diamond mining, with large-scale production commencing in South Africa in the early 20th century. Historically, Africa has played a pivotal role in the diamond trade, producing 98% of the world's diamonds between 1889 and 1959.
<br/>
However, diamonds from African conflict zones have given rise to the term "blood diamonds," which were used to fund insurgency and crime. This dark side of the diamond trade has prompted international efforts, such as the Kimberley Process, to ensure ethical sourcing and trade of diamonds.

# ***The Most Prevalent Forms of Cyber Crime***
<br/>
<Image src="/30870.jpeg" alt="" width={700} height={650} />
<br />
In recent years, cybercrime has surged, driven in part by the COVID-19 pandemic's acceleration of the shift from offline to online activities. In 2022, global losses from cybercrime were estimated at a staggering $7.1 trillion, a significant increase from $1.2 trillion in 2019. Notably, state-affiliated hacking groups like Lazarus, associated with North Korea, have been responsible for crypto exchange and protocol hacks, contributing to the rising losses.
<br />
The landscape of cyberattacks has evolved significantly over the past five years. In 2017, around 42 percent of recorded cybercrimes were related to non-payment or non-delivery, including fraudulent online purchases and failed promised payments. Personal data breaches and phishing scams made up an additional 28 percent, while identity theft and credit card fraud had a smaller share.
<br />
However, the most prevalent cyberattack method today is phishing, accounting for over half of all criminal online activity. Phishing has diversified beyond email-based attacks to include spear phishing, targeting specific groups or roles within organizations, and whaling, which focuses on top executives. Additionally, other forms of phishing, like smishing (via text messages) and vishing (via voice calls), have emerged.
<br />
This surge in cybercrime underscores the need for heightened cybersecurity measures across individuals, businesses, and governments. As cybercriminals adapt and employ increasingly sophisticated tactics, vigilance and robust cybersecurity strategies are essential to mitigate the risks and financial losses associated with cyberattacks. It is clear that as the digital landscape continues to evolve, the battle against cybercrime will remain a critical and ongoing challenge for society.
