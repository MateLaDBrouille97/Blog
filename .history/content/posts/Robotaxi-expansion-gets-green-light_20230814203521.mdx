Two leading "robotaxi" companies, Waymo and Cruise, have received approval to charge for their driverless car services across San Francisco. This represents a significant expansion for these companies, as they had previously been restricted in terms of when and where they could charge for rides. The decision comes despite objections from various quarters, including police, fire officials, and activists. This move is seen as a potential turning point for the driverless car industry, with Waymo and Cruise poised to capitalize on this opportunity.
<br/>
Waymo, a subsidiary of Google's parent company, expressed that this marks the true beginning of their commercial operations in San Francisco. Cruise, a General Motors subsidiary, referred to this development as a "historic industry milestone" and has predicted substantial revenue growth from this venture.
<br/>
Driverless car services have faced technological challenges, safety concerns, and notable accidents that hindered their transformational promises made over a decade ago. The approval was granted by the California Public Utilities Commission, with some concerns raised about safety records and traffic incidents caused by the technology.
<br/>
Waymo and Cruise both plan a gradual expansion of their services despite strong demand. Waymo currently serves over 10,000 rides per week with its driverless cars in San Francisco and Phoenix. Cruise operates in multiple cities and aims to begin service in Texas cities as well. This decision is being hailed as trailblazing in the self-driving technology sector, seen as a step toward developing sustainable business models and accelerating the benefits of autonomous vehicles for people everywhere.

Online auctions like A Better Bid are adapting to this change, offering a new way to bid on a wide array of EVs.

Alphabet's Waymo is set to expand its autonomous ride-hailing service to Austin, Texas, adding to its existing presence in three other US cities. The expansion reinforces Waymo's focus on its Waymo One ride-hailing platform, which operates with an all-electric fleet, contributing to environmental sustainability.
<br/>
The announcement, made on Waymo's blog, introduces Austin as the company's fourth location, with testing already underway in the downtown area. The Waymo One service employs an all-electric fleet powered by Jaguar I-PACE vehicles, offering mapping and autonomous capabilities to the public.
<br/>
Saswat Panigrahi, Chief Product Officer at Waymo, commended Austin's cityscape adaptability for autonomous driving. Waymo's strategic move to concentrate on Waymo One's development and expansion is reflected in its partnership with DTNA taking a back seat in favor of enhancing public services. Austin's selection highlights Waymo's commitment to furthering its service coverage.
<br/>
Waymo's journey began in San Francisco, initially competing with GM's Cruise in pioneering autonomous ride-hailing. Subsequently, both companies expanded to other cities, including Metro Phoenix. Waymo's influential partnership with Uber also enabled its autonomous vehicles to be hailed through the Uber app.
<br/>
This expansion underscores Waymo's dedication to advancing its autonomous technology and Waymo One service, extending its reach to Austin, Texas, a major US city, for piloting and mapping its autonomous ride-hailing service.
