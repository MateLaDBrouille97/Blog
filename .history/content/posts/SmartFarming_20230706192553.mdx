# What is Smart farming
<br/>
The Internet of Things (IoT) is revolutionizing the agricultural industry through the concept of smart farming. Smart farming involves using modern technologies to manage farms, increase productivity, and optimize labor. This approach utilizes various tools and technologies such as sensors for monitoring soil, water, light, humidity, and temperature, specialized software solutions, connectivity options like cellular and LoRa, GPS and satellite location tracking, robotics for autonomous tractors and processing facilities, and data analytics for analyzing farm data.
<br/>
<Image src="/smartfarming.png" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br />
In the IoT-based smart farming cycle, sensors collect observational data, which is then processed by cloud-hosted IoT platforms to diagnose the condition of crops, livestock, soil, or atmosphere. Based on this information, decisions are made on whether location-specific treatment is required, and appropriate actions are taken. This cycle repeats continuously to ensure efficient farming practices.
<br/>
IoT solutions in agriculture address various challenges and offer significant benefits. Precision farming allows for precise treatment of plants and animals by making decisions on a per-square-meter or per-plant/animal basis. This improves the effectiveness of pesticides and fertilizers, reducing waste and optimizing resources. Precision livestock farming enables better monitoring of individual animals' needs, preventing diseases and enhancing herd health. Automation in smart greenhouses eliminates manual intervention by using IoT to monitor and control climate conditions, resulting in higher production efficiency.
<br/>
Agricultural drones are used for crop health assessment, irrigation, monitoring, spraying, and analysis. The data collected by drones provides valuable insights into various metrics, enabling farmers to make informed decisions. Moreover, IoT-based smart farming can be applied to organic farming, family farming, and other emerging trends, enhancing transparency and sustainability in agricultural practices.
<br/>
<Image src="/iot-smart-farming-solutions.jpg" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br />
Smart farming, driven by IoT and other technologies, represents the Third Green Revolution in agriculture. It optimizes resource utilization, reduces the use of pesticides and fertilizers, improves food traceability and safety, and promotes sustainable farming practices. With a rapidly growing population, smart farming has the potential to ensure a productive and sustainable future for agriculture.

# How Automation Is Shaping the Future of Agriculture
<br/>
Smart farming, also known as Farming 4.0 or farm automation, is revolutionizing the agriculture industry through the adoption of advanced technologies. The world population is expected to reach 9.7 billion by 2050, necessitating a 70% increase in agricultural production to meet the growing food demand. However, the industry also faces the challenge of doing so sustainably, considering the significant environmental impact of food production. Additionally, labor shortages due to urban migration and an aging population pose another obstacle for farmers.
<br/>
<Image src="/smart-farming-transforming-agriculture.png" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br />
Smart farming addresses these challenges by leveraging technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT), drones, robotics, and artificial intelligence (AI) to optimize food production processes. It enables farmers to remotely monitor field conditions and manage operations from a central location, improving efficiency and productivity. Precision farming, a subset of smart farming, focuses on precise measurements of livestock and land conditions to make informed decisions and optimize agricultural processes. Digital farming, on the other hand, utilizes existing data to derive actionable intelligence and increase productivity throughout the agri-food value chain.
<br/>
The benefits of smart farming are numerous. It enables water conservation through the use of soil- and weather-related sensors, reduces operational costs by automating processes, and increases production rates and quality through optimized crop treatment. Real-time data and insights empower farmers to make informed decisions, while GPS tracking and sensors aid in monitoring livestock health. Smart farming also contributes to reducing the environmental footprint of agriculture, accurately evaluating farm and field performance, and enabling remote monitoring and equipment management.
<br/>
<Image src="/Smartfarming.png" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br />
The implementation of smart farming practices helps farmers make the most of available resources, minimize environmental impact, and improve food security on a global scale. The widespread adoption of these technologies is shaping the future of agriculture, allowing farmers to overcome the challenges posed by population growth, sustainability requirements, and labor shortages.

<br/>

<br />
Technological advancements and the adoption of innovative technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), blockchain, and machine learning are key drivers for the growth of the smart agriculture market. These technologies provide farmers with efficient crop-growing methods and create lucrative opportunities for market development.
<br/>
Government initiatives in various regions are also contributing to market growth. Governments in developing and developed countries are promoting the adoption of advanced technologies in agriculture and offering tax benefits to market players. Additionally, governments are investing heavily in the smart agriculture sector.

<br/>
The increasing adoption of smartphones and growth in the telecom sector are driving market growth. Smartphones help farmers track and monitor agriculture activities, provide accurate information and data, and assist in record-keeping.

### *** Smart farming using artificial intelligence: Review***
<br/>
Smart farming with artificial intelligence, specifically machine learning, deep learning, and time series analysis, offers efficient solutions to address the challenges of agricultural sustainability. These techniques are utilized in various processes such as crop selection, crop yield prediction, soil compatibility classification, water management, and more. Machine learning algorithms are employed for crop selection and management, deep learning techniques for crop selection and production forecasting, and time series analysis for demand forecasting, commodity price prediction, and crop yield forecasting.
<br/>
The article provides a comprehensive review of machine learning and deep learning techniques in the agricultural industry. Machine learning algorithms are utilized to classify soil fertility, recommend suitable crops based on soil properties, and make crop management decisions. Deep learning techniques, such as Extreme Learning Machine (ELM) and radial basis function networks (RBFN), are explored for crop selection. Time series analysis methods like Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) are reviewed for crop yield prediction.
<br/>
The increasing population necessitates accurate crop production forecasting to ensure food sufficiency. The article emphasizes the importance of applying machine learning and deep learning to predict crop yield and make informed decisions to enhance production. By utilizing time series analysis, crop yield can be forecasted, and appropriate crop recommendations can be made, thereby mitigating food insufficiency.
<br/>
The review also highlights the challenges in implementing smart farming, particularly the need to consider various factors such as climate, topography, and institutional factors that impact agriculture. The article suggests that advanced machine learning algorithms can address these challenges and improve crop selection and management.
<br/>
The article underscores the significance of smart farming with machine learning, deep learning, and time series analysis in enhancing agricultural practices. By leveraging these techniques, farmers can make informed decisions, increase crop yield, and mitigate food insufficiency. The review provides valuable insights into the application of different algorithms and suggests future research directions to further advance smart farming in the agricultural sector.

### ***Brazil Agtech Market Map***
<br/>
Brazil has become a major player in the global agricultural industry, transitioning from a net food importer to the second largest agricultural exporter in the world. The country's agribusiness sector accounts for approximately 50% of its exports and 23% of GDP. Contrary to common belief, the success of Brazil's agricultural sector is not solely due to government support and deforestation of the Amazon. In fact, government support for agriculture in Brazil has been relatively low compared to other countries, and the core agricultural growth occurs far away from the rainforest.
<br/>
<Image src="/BrazilianAGTech.jpg" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br />
Scientific and technological advancements have played a significant role in Brazil's agricultural success. The Brazilian Agricultural Research Corporation (EMBRAPA) has developed numerous innovations that have transformed previously unfarmable land into productive agricultural regions. For example, the Cerrado region was made suitable for large-scale livestock and grain production through the application of lime and the breeding of bacteria to improve soil conditions. The University of São Paulo's Escola Superior de Agricultura Luiz de Queiroz (ESALQ) has also contributed to agricultural research and development.
<br/>
Piracicaba, a city in São Paulo state, is a hub of Brazil's agtech ecosystem. It is home to the AgTech Garage, an important agtech hub and think tank in Latin America, as well as a science park and startup incubator. The improving technological infrastructure in Brazil, including connectivity and digital literacy, has facilitated the growth of the agtech ecosystem. Smartphone penetration has increased significantly, enabling agtech companies to bring new products and solutions to farmers more efficiently.
<br/>
The agtech startup landscape in Brazil can be divided into three segments: "Before The Farm," "Inside The Farm," and "After The Farm." Startups in these segments operate in various areas of the agricultural supply chain, including inputs manufacturing, farm management software, scouting and pest monitoring, distribution and trading, and food innovations. The quality and quantity of agtech startups in Brazil have seen significant growth, attracting talented entrepreneurs and investors.
<br/>
Overall, Brazil's agtech ecosystem is thriving, driven by a combination of scientific advancements, technological infrastructure, entrepreneurial talent, and investor interest. The country's agricultural sector continues to play a vital role in its economy and global food production.

Precision agriculture involves capturing and analyzing data from various sources such as soil type, nutrient levels, and topography to optimize profitability and sustainability while protecting the environment.

### ***How Agritech Startups Are Rejuvenating Indonesia’s Agriculture Sector***
<br/>
In recent years, Indonesia has faced challenges in its agricultural sector, with a decline in the number of farmers due to factors such as land scarcity, limited access to capital, and volatile market prices. To address these issues, agritech startups have emerged to provide innovative solutions in the agricultural sector.
<br/>
One of the main challenges for Indonesian farmers is gaining access to the market. The country's archipelagic nature makes it difficult for farmers to transport their goods directly to end-consumers. Agritech startups like TaniHub and SayurBox have developed smartphone apps to connect growers with consumers, cutting out the middlemen in the supply chain. This not only helps farmers increase their income but also reduces prices for consumers. Other startups like Panen ID and Chilibeli's Chilimart facilitate direct trade with hotels and restaurants, focusing on the business-to-business (B2B) market.
<br/>
<Image src="/agritech-landscape.png" alt="" width={800} height={700} />
<br/>
Access to capital is another obstacle for farmers, as they often struggle to secure financing from traditional banks. To bridge this gap, agritech startups have incorporated elements of fintech into their products. For example, TaniHub launched TaniFund, a peer-to-peer lending platform that allows users to lend money to farmers and earn high returns.
<br/>
Technological innovation in the agricultural sector has also seen advancements, particularly in farming Internet of Things (IoT) technologies. Startups like Habibi Garden, MSMB, eFishery, and Jala have developed tools and devices to automate and standardize farming practices, ranging from automated watering systems to smart fish feeding solutions.
<br/>
Despite the presence of existing agritech startups, there are still untapped opportunities in the agriculture space, as different segments within the sector require specifically tailored solutions. Additionally, agricultural solutions are highly localized, requiring a deep understanding of the local operational challenges such as logistics and transportation. This presents an open space for new businesses to establish hyper-local agriculture product marketplaces, especially in rural areas.
